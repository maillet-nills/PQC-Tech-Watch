## Description

**NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology)** is a U.S. government agency that acts as the world's trusted authority on cryptographic standards. Think of them as the "referees" who decide which encryption methods are safe for protecting everything from government secrets to your banking data.

In plain terms: When a new encryption method is invented, NIST tests it, breaks it down, verifies it's secure, and then says "yes, this is safe to use" or "no, this needs more work." Once NIST approves something, banks, governments, and tech companies trust it and use it.

## Why NIST matters right now:

The world's encryption is being threatened by quantum computers (computers that will be able to crack today's security in minutes). NIST has been working since 2016 to create new, quantum-proof encryption methods. In 2024, they officially approved the first batch of these quantum-safe algorithms. This is a major milestone—it means companies can now start protecting themselves against this future threat.

## Why it's relevant

**Understanding the bigger picture of post-quantum cryptography:**

1. **They're the official decision-makers** : When governments and companies decide to adopt quantum-safe encryption, they follow NIST's recommendations. If you see a company like Thales (or the U.S. government under Trump) announcing PQC adoption, they're implementing what NIST approved.

2. **Recent milestone (2024)** : NIST just finalized new encryption standards that are quantum-safe. This moves PQC from "theoretical research" to "ready for real use", which is why you're seeing more news about it now.

3. **Connects the dots** : This tech watch monitors news about PQC adoption and deployment. NIST is the "why" behind all of it. Governments and companies are adopting because NIST gave them the green light.

4. **Future-proofing** : If you work in cybersecurity, finance, government, or any field handling sensitive data, understanding NIST's roadmap helps you see what's coming.
